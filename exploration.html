Choose What You Hear

Welcome, co-scientist. This is the interactive hub for **Project Stillness**. Our goal is to move beyond passive noise cancellation and pioneer **active auditory selection**. This application is our shared workspace to explore the core ideas, compare potential directions, and decide how we'll empower users to shape their own auditory world.

Three Paths to Stillness

Here are the three initial avenues we've identified. Each represents a unique approach to achieving auditory control. Click on any path to expand its details and explore the underlying mechanics, use cases, and challenges.

The Auditory Filter

Focuses on real-time sound manipulation, allowing users to mute or amplify specific sounds in their environment.

How It Works

A microphone captures ambient sound, and an AI model identifies sources (speech, traffic, music). The user interface provides controls to adjust the volume of each identified category.

Example Use Case

A student in a noisy cafe mutes conversations while keeping the background music audible for focus.

Key Challenge

Requires significant processing power for accurate, real-time sound separation without latency.

Personalized Soundscape

Focuses on generative audio, creating unique soundscapes tailored to the user's mood or desired mental state.

How It Works

The system uses simple text prompts (e.g., "relax," "focus") or biometric data to generate non-repeating, ambient audio based on principles of music theory and psychoacoustics.

Example Use Case

An artist seeking a flow state requests a soundscape that is "calm but with a feeling of forward motion."

Key Challenge

Designing a generative algorithm that can produce audio that is consistently meaningful and non-monotonous.

The Sonic Annotator

Focuses on user perception, allowing people to map and understand their personal reactions to their sound environment.

How It Works

Users "tag" sounds in their environment as positive, negative, or neutral. Over time, the app builds a personal "sound map," revealing patterns in their auditory experience and sensitivities.

Example Use Case

A person with sensory sensitivity identifies which specific sounds are most distressing, empowering them to avoid or mitigate them.

Key Challenge

Creating an intuitive and engaging interface that makes the act of sonic annotation feel natural and rewarding.
